Biden tells Netanyahu Gaza ceasefire deal is urgent

國際新聞（英文原文）

US President Joe Biden has "stressed the urgency" of reaching a Gaza ceasefire and hostage release deal to Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, the White House has said. In a call on Wednesday, which was also joined by Vice President Kamala Harris, Mr Biden is said to have stated the importance of removing any remaining obstacles blocking an agreement with Hamas.

He also reaffirmed Washington's commitment to help defend Israel against what the White House called "all threats from Iran, including its proxy terrorist groups Hamas, Hezbollah, and the Houthis". This unequivocal support comes amid growing international pressure for a ceasefire.

It came after the US secretary of state wrapped up a diplomatic tour of the Middle East, pushing for a deal that could end the war. The protracted conflict has led to a humanitarian crisis in Gaza, with widespread destruction and loss of life.

The office of Mr Netanyahu reiterated on Wednesday that Israel planned to keep troops in a strip of land along the border between Gaza and Egypt - known as the Philadelphi Corridor - in the event of any such deal. This stance has become a significant point of contention in negotiations.

"Israel will insist on the achievement of all of its objectives for the war, as they have been defined by the security cabinet, including that Gaza never again constitutes a security threat to Israel. This requires securing the southern border," a statement said. The intransigence of both sides has made reaching an agreement challenging.

The impasse in negotiations has led to mounting frustration among international mediators. Egypt and Qatar, key interlocutors in the talks, have expressed concern over the lack of progress. The protracted nature of the conflict has also led to increased scrutiny of Israel's military tactics and the proportionality of its response to Hamas's initial attack.

As the situation continues to deteriorate, the international community is calling for an immediate ceasefire and unhindered access for　humanitarian aid. The plight of civilians caught in the crossfire remains a primary concern, with many facing acute shortages of food, water, and medical supplies.
